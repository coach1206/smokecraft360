1. The Wrapper (Capa)
The outermost leaf that encases the cigar body. It provides visual appeal and contributes a significant percentage of the initial flavor profile due to concentrated essential oils. Key varieties include:
 Connecticut Shade: Light straw-colored, grown under protective cloth tents. Delivers smooth, low-tannin characteristics with notes of cream, cedar, and mild white pepper.
 Maduro: Dark, thick leaves subjected to prolonged high-temperature fermentation cycles. Delivers rich sweetness, dark cocoa, espresso bean, and molasses characteristics.
 Habano: Darker, reddish-brown leaf grown from Cuban-seed stocks. Known for intense spice, leather, wood, and robust earthy undertones.
2. The Binder (Capote)
The structural leaf layer directly beneath the wrapper. It contains the filler leaves, maintains the cigar's shape, and regulates the burn rate to ensure even combustion.
3. The Filler (Tripa)
The core blend of tobacco leaves inside the cigar, responsible for its overall strength and flavor complexity.
 Ligero: Leaves harvested from the top of the tobacco plant. They receive the most sunlight, producing thick, oily leaves that burn slowly and deliver high nicotine content and intense spice flavors.
 Seco: Mild leaves harvested from the middle section of the plant. They offer balanced, nuanced flavors and provide excellent combustion properties.
 Volado: Thin leaves from the base of the plant. They have minimal flavor impact but provide the burning qualities necessary to keep the cigar lit evenly.
